Due to further advances in robotics, engineers have now been able to program a robot to do a menial household chore like folding clothes. It works by the robot being able to work out the geometry of the article, and computing various algorithms which will give it a sequence of movements which, when carried out, will result in a folded item.

There are folding methods that are definitely more popular than others, however, many different techniques such as the Japanese method, were also researched and taken into mind when working out what the ideal way of folding would be. Due to the physical complications, it was preferable to look at the problem from a geometric point of view when defining a cloth model. From the geometry of the article, the number of grippers required is worked out.

Of course some restrictions and assumptions had to be made for the job to be feasible. Some part of the material has to be in contact with the flat surface at all times. Furthermore, after it grips, it raises the material such that it becomes perpendicular with the surface. The motion of the robot is restricted in this way, and uses gravity to reduce the number of grippers required.

  • The cloth has infinite flexibility. There is no energy contribution from bending.
  • The cloth is non-stretchable. No geodesic path lengths can be increased.
  • The cloth has infinite friction with the surface on which it lies and with itself.
  • The cloth has zero thickness.
  • The cloth is subject to gravity.
  • The cloth has no dynamics.(1)

A further assumption regarding the movement of the cloth was that, when one or more grippers release the cloth, there will be no upward movement of the cloth anywhere due to gravity and the internal forces of the cloth. This is called the downward tendency assumption.(2)

For a flat, simple rectangular-shaped item, like a towel, the process is more straightforward. The robot can identify the object in question by using background segmentation. This means the robot just sees the space that the object is occupying. The rest, as far as the robot is concerned, is not there. Next, it is easy for the robot to work out where the corners of the towel are, and so it uses the grippers to pick them up and begin folding in half repeatedly until the towel is at the required size.(3)

The process is much more difficult with items such as T-shirts. The grippers must be gripping the cloth in such a way as to make the vertical section immobilised. This is to ensure the deterministic behaviour of the cloth. The points on the flat surface are already immobilised as they cannot move due to both friction and downward-tendency assumption. This means we do not need to grasp them at these points. The aim is to make all parts of the cloth immobilised. We do this using the theorem:

In our material model, a vertically hanging cloth polygon is immobilised when every convex vertex of the cloth at which the negative gravity vector does not point into the cloth polygon is fixed (i.e. be held by a gripper or be part of the baseline).

Now we can remove all the immobilised points and use the theorem again on the remainder of the cloth. It recurses on the convex vertices of the new polygon. These vertices were non-convex in the original cloth.(4)

The way the robot folds the material is by a series of g-folds. A g-fold is a when you define a line whose endpoints are on the boundary of the cloth. Then you fold the left side of the line over the line until it lies horizontally on top of the other part whilst maintaining this property: At all times during a folding procedure, every part of the cloth is either horizontal or vertical, and the grippers hold points on the vertical part such that it is immobilised.(5)

Once the exact motion of the grippers has been programmed, we can then start folding items such as shirts or trousers. When looking at a new article of clothing, a human clicks on the vertices of the item. Then, using a GUI, the user can click to specify the sequence of g-folds the robot can perform with the grippers. This is done one fold at a time.

If a specified fold is not achievable, then the user is able to call a folding primitive. The sequence of folds for the primitives are parametrised on features including scaling, rotation and side lengths. For the purposes of keeping experiments consistent, these primitives are used.
